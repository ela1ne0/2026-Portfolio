Nebraska Flood Awareness Initiative

The Challenge

Flood data is typically presented through high-density GIS visualizations that assume technical literacy. For many rural residents — particularly those with limited digital access — these maps can feel overwhelming or inaccessible. The challenge was twofold: translate complex geospatial flood data into clear, human-centered visuals, and ensure the system met public-sector accessibility standards. The information was urgent, but it couldn't feel alarming.

Research Foundation

Alongside the design work, I studied flood recovery patterns using GIS and deep learning models (Siamese U-Net), contributing to a peer-reviewed publication in Environment and Planning B: Urban Analytics and City Science (2026). The research informed how recovery trajectories differ across communities and how to visually represent patterns without oversimplifying risk.

Strategy & Execution

  • Print-First Accessibility: Designed postcard-style outreach materials mirroring the web interface for communities with limited broadband.
  • AI-Assisted Iteration: Incorporated GPT-4 and DALL·E into early drafts with human oversight, reducing iteration time by ~30%.

The Impact

  • 8,000+ Residents Reached across 59 Nebraska communities.
  • Adopted by HUD-Related Stakeholders for disaster recovery support initiatives.
  • Peer-Reviewed Research Contribution on flood recovery visualization.

What I'd do differently

I'd invest more time in field testing the postcard materials with community partners before finalizing the visual system, and document accessibility decisions more explicitly for public-sector handoff.
